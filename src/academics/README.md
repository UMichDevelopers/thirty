# Academics

The first day of class is August 31.

## Terms

* **Fall** and **Winter** are the two main terms, each
  lasting approximately 14 weeks. We typically begin in the Fall term.

* **Spring** and **Summer** are half-terms. You likely don't need to worry
  about these.

## Courses and scheduling

Your schedule in college is determined by yourself. You will have a set of
requirements that you must meet to graduate, but beyond that you are free to
take whatever you want, whenever you want.

## Orientation and advising

After you pay your enrollment deposit, you will be emailed a page to register
for orientation. Orientation consists of two parts:

- Meet with your first-year advisor over the summer to register for your Fall
  2026 classes. **Classes are first-come, first-serve.** This means selecting an
  early orientation date is beneficial.

- An in-person orientation day on-campus. If you're out of state, or
  international, I suggest you pick one closer to the end of August so you
  don't need to make a dedicated visit just for orientation.

Please speak with an advisor (preferably a departmental one) to discuss your
academic plans and anytime you make a significant change to your plans. Try to
speak with them at least once a semester to make sure you're on track and
nothing catches you by surprise later.

## Have a backup plan

Course registration technically starts on 30 March, so you will see
availability decrease as class-groups above you select their courses,
especially if you're trying to take courses beyond introductory level.

Nothing you "plan" is concrete until the hour of your registration timeslot,
and even then if you are really unlucky you might watch the last spot disappear
before your eyes.

Always be prepared with not just backup sections but also backup courses,
especially as a freshman; if an upperclassman for some reason wants to stake
out some spots in intro courses that open up in July, they could.

## Graduation requirements

Each college/school has a set of general education requirements. These are
courses that every student has to take regardless of major. See, for example,
[LSA](https://admissions.umich.edu/summary-lsa-degree-requirements) and
[Engineering](https://bulletin.engin.umich.edu/ug-ed/reqs/).

You also need to consult the requirements for your major.

## Prerequisites 

Courses generally have something called an "advisory" or "enforced"
prerequisite. Advisory means you don't have to have it, but it is highly
recommended. Enforced means you must have it.

## Credits

There's no need to try to take 18 credits; you might even overload yourself
by doing that. And there certainly is no point is going over 18 credits and
paying extra.

## Important deadlines

You should note your important academic deadlines (examples from
[LSA](https://lsa.umich.edu/lsa/academics/dates-and-deadlines/lsa-academic-calendar.html)
and [Engineering](https://bulletin.engin.umich.edu/academic-calendar-and-deadlines/undergraduate-deadlines/))
such as:

- Last day to drop/swap courses without taking a W (withdrawal) on your
  transcript.

- Last day to withdraw from a course with a W or Pass/Fail. Note that this is
  before final exams, meaning if you're in a situation where you're barely
  passing, then you need to calculate how well you expect to do on the final;
  because if you bomb the final, you're out of luck.

Also, deadlines in college are generally really deadlines, with much less
flexibility than you might be used to in high school.

## Class policies

Whether or not your class requires attendance and/or is recorded for the entire
class cannot be known for sure until you get your syllabus. Instructors change,
and even the same instructor might change their policies. So don't go in with
an expectation that you can skip a class and just watch recordings. A decent
amount of instructors are only releasing recordings to those with valid
reasons.

### Sites for courses and scheduling

- [Atlas](https://atlas.ai.umich.edu/) contains lists of courses, grade
  distributions, workload, and occasionally links to the syllabus.
  Keep in mind that this data may not be 100% accurate depending on your
  specific section of the class and different instructors. Workload is often
  judged by students in the context of other courses of equivalent credit,
  meaning looking at the workload for a 1 credit class vs a 4 credit class is
  not a good comparison since 4 credit classes would almost always have
  "higher" workload to begin with.

- The [Zenith Scheduler](https://zenithscheduler.com) is purpose-built for U-M
  students and can help you build a reasonable timetable from your desired
  courses.

- If you are in LSA, consider also using the [LSA Course
  Guide](https://webapps.lsa.umich.edu/cg/).
